Books on Tape

Generative Art

As a visual artist my hand is a pathetic tool. I cannot draw, paint, or sculpt-- the things I create never match what is in my mind's eye. The emergence of generative image models offered a unique artistic outlet for me. Steering visual output with words directly appealed to me, as I had resigned myself to writing being my only marginally competent artistic medium. Being able to create visual output from language input was immediately captivating. And my archivist/librarian instincts turned my knowledge of hundreds of niche artists into a prompt palette. My artistic practice with generative image models involves exploration of the artist profiles that models recognize and combining the aesthetics of distinct creators from history to produce new sensible intermediary points between them in latent space.

Lunacy

I have always been drawn to the word Lunacy. It sounds beautiful, and its connection to the Moon and Insanity resonates with me. My first real series of generative pieces took my artist palette approach and applied it to this word. Below are some highlights from this series. On the left are sample images from the artists that I combined in a prompt to produce the image on the right. The specific images I chose by each artist have nothing explicit to do with the output image, they were just selected as an attempt to be representative of the aesthetic profile learned by the model.

Composition 8 (1923)

Wasily Kandinsky (1866-1944)

Wassily Kandinsky was a Russian painter and art theorist, known for his pivotal role in the development of abstract art. Associated with the Blue Rider and Bauhaus movements, Kandinsky's art is characterized by vibrant colors, geometric shapes, and spiritual symbolism. His innovative use of abstract forms aimed to evoke emotions and spiritual experiences, influencing generations of artists and shaping the course of modern art.

Bandits' Roost, 59 1/2 Mulberry Street. (1888)

Jacob August Riis (1849-1914)

Jacob August Riis was a Danish-American social reformer and photojournalist, renowned for his pioneering work in documentary photography. Based in New York City, Riis documented the living conditions of immigrants and the urban poor in the late 19th century. His photograph series', such as "How the Other Half Lives," exposed the harsh realities of tenement life, prompting social reforms and raising awareness about poverty and inequality. Riis' impactful visual storytelling continues to inspire documentary photographers and advocates for social change.

Un soir sur la Loire (1923)

Félix Vallotton (1865-1925)

Félix Vallotton was a Swiss-French painter and printmaker associated with the Les Nabis group. Known for his distinctive use of flat colors and bold compositions, Vallotton's art often explored themes of introspection, psychological tension, and everyday life. His sharp contrasts and simplified forms influenced the development of modern graphic art and contributed to the rise of woodcut as a fine art medium. Vallotton's work continues to be celebrated for its innovative approach and lasting impact on early 20th-century art.

Somber and Hard (1927)

Oscar Bluemner (1867-1938)

Oscar Bluemner (1867-1938) was a German-American painter and architect best known for his vibrant and geometrically structured landscapes. Bluemner's art fused elements of Cubism and Expressionism, using bold colors and dynamic compositions to capture the essence of American landscapes and urban scenes. His architectural background informed his distinctive use of space and form, creating compositions that explored the interplay between man-made structures and natural environments. Bluemner's innovative approach to color and form influenced later American modernist painters and continues to be admired for its unique visual language.
