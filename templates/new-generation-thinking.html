Introduction

In my opening essay on Substack, I tried to communicate a little bit about who I am, but it has come to my attention that no similar piece of writing exists covering what exactly it is that I am trying to achieve with this project. And that is partially by design. This project has always been— and hopefully always will be— my personal vehicle for learning and self-expression. I don’t yet fully understand the passions that motivate me, so to claim to know exactly what I hope my audience gets out of my content would be a statement of tremendous hubris. I have intangible goals that I have been working to translate into words since the beginning of my page, but even as I write up this essay, many of those goals still elude my ability to capture them in English. So I will begin with the pieces that I can communicate, and hopefully as the years go by, my aims will continue to grow clearer. In this essay I want to focus on why I chose the name A New Generation of Memes.

A New Generation

It matters not where you live, or what rank of life you hold, the evil or the blessing will reach you all. The far and the near, the home counties and the back, the rich and the poor, will suffer or rejoice alike. The heart that feels not now is dead; the blood of his children will curse his cowardice, who shrinks back at a time when a little might have saved the whole, and made them happy. I love the man that can smile in trouble, that can gather strength from distress, and grow brave by reflection. 'Tis the business of little minds to shrink; but he whose heart is firm, and whose conscience approves his conduct, will pursue his principles unto death.

Thomas Paine, The Crisis, December 23, 1776
In 1776, the world was awash with a new type of content— the pamphlet. There was an excitement in the air. The invention of the printing press, and its subsequent widespread proliferation, resulted in entirely new types of written work developing and spreading among the people of the Western world. The new short form written content was available to far more people than had previously been allowed to comment on intellectual affairs, and the impact on social, political, and religious structures was profound, and— historically speaking— effectively immediate.

Thomas Paine and his contemporaries, often forgoing personal reward, traditional prestige, and their own physical safety, prolifically published essay after essay trying to encourage the American public to rise to the challenge of their age. It was their tireless efforts to “go viral” that inspired the poor and rich alike to break from the British and fight for a freedom previously unknown to Western society. These weren’t the actions and words of the next generation of British colonial leaders— they were the rallying cry for a completely new generation of American leaders.

The name A New Generation of Memes was born out of this idea that the subtle difference between the phrase “next generation” and “new generation” matters immensely. The phrase “next generation” always seems to place humans in relation to some larger system. Next generation of politicians, next generation of doctors, next generation of celebrities, next generation of activists. It subtly presupposes a continuation of the status quo— a series of humans cycling through an unchanging system. It emphasizes analytical, organizational, bureaucratic minds. It puts cognitive limits on our ambition, our spirit, and our conception of what is possible. This “next generation” of leaders are necessary to keep the world functioning, but their strengths alone are not enough to handle the technological revolution that we are living through.

Conversely, it seems to me that the phrase “new generation” centers the truly unique experience of every generation of humans. It conveys a sense of freshness and risk that is liberating, but heavy with responsibility. It reminds us to take pride in the uniqueness of our experience, but not to take for granted the institutions and traditions that will only survive if we uphold them.

But my own subjective opinions can only mean so much. To investigate the phrases’ usage in English language more broadly, I used OpenAI’s GPT-4 Large Language Model (LLM). The model is trained on a widespread, diverse, and extensive pile of English language, and, to be wildly over-generalizing, it uses Family Feud style probability to determine what it says word by word. This felt like a perspective from a higher-level view of the two phrases than my own, so I present the next part of this essay as a slightly stylized dialogue resulting from those conversations.

"Next generation" suggests a more direct link to the previous generation, emphasizing continuity and progression. The use of the word "next" indicates an orderly sequence of generations, with each generation building upon the foundations laid by its predecessor. In contrast, "new generation" places greater emphasis on the element of change and refreshment in relation to the previous generation. The choice between these two phrases often influences how we perceive the relationship between generations. When discussing the "next generation," it is natural to consider how that generation is fulfilling the promise and potential of its predecessor, perhaps in fields such as technology, science, or societal progress. The emphasis is on the linear progression of human knowledge and achievement.

In contrast, when we talk about a "new generation," we might focus more on the generational divide or the differences that define each group. The discussion might revolve around the unique challenges or opportunities faced by the new generation, as well as the ways they diverge from their forebears in terms of values, beliefs, and priorities.

In essence, the connotations of "next generation" reflect an emphasis on uninterrupted progress and interdependence between generations, while "new generation" highlights the distinctiveness, innovation, and potential for change that each generation embodies. These subtle differences in meaning can shape our understanding of how generations relate to one another and influence the broader narrative of human development.
Me: That is very much in line with my own thinking and limited observations. If you had to apply one phrase to machine intelligence and one phrase to human intelligence, which phrase would you say fits each best?

When considering "next generation" and "new generation" in the context of machine intelligence and human intelligence, an interesting distinction can be drawn.

For machine intelligence, the phrase "next generation" seems more fitting. This is because machine intelligence, such as AI and machine learning, evolves in a more linear and predictable fashion. It is built upon previous iterations and generations of technology, with each generation being an improvement or extension of the previous one. The progression of machine intelligence tends to be driven by human engineering and innovation, and is thus fashioned as a series of advancements in a sequential, step-by-step manner.

In contrast, human intelligence is better described as a "new generation." Human intelligence is shaped by a multitude of factors such as genetics, environment, upbringing, and socio-cultural contexts, which makes each generation of humans distinct and diverse in their thinking, behavior, and perspectives. While humans inherit some traits and knowledge from previous generations, they are also influenced by current events, new ideas, and shifting societal trends. Consequently, each new generation of humans has the potential to bring about significant changes in the way society functions, creating fresh opportunities for growth, exploration, and development.

In summary, "next generation" fits machine intelligence more aptly, as it represents the linear and progressive nature of technological advancement, while "new generation" is a better descriptor for human intelligence, as it highlights the uniqueness, adaptability, and change inherent in each generation of people.

The terms "next generation" and "new generation" are often used in philosophical, sociological, and political discourse to talk about progress. The progression of ideas, values, standards, and knowledge is often tied to generational shifts, signifying a transition from old to new, the past to the future.

"Next generation" typically refers to the immediate successors of the current generation. In the context of progress, it suggests a temporal sequence and continuity. It emphasizes the idea of progression as an incremental process happening over time. The term implies that improvements and advancements should be built on the knowledge and achievements of the current generation. It embodies the belief in linear progress, wherein successive generations make steady advancements on what has come before. This perspective aligns with Enlightenment philosophers like Immanuel Kant who believed in the perfectibility of human nature and societal progress, implying each succeeding generation can and should strive to improve upon its predecessor.

On the other hand, "new generation" has a connotation of change and innovation. It implies not just a chronological successor, but a fresh start. In relation to progress, it suggests the possibility of radical shifts or transformations. The term resonates with the concept of progress as revolution, where a new generation can bring about significant changes that diverge from or even challenge the status quo. It embodies the potential for breakthrough developments and paradigm shifts. This aligns with Thomas Kuhn's concept of paradigm shifts or revolutionary science, reflecting the idea that progress is not always gradual but can occur in leaps and bounds with new concepts, approaches, or technologies that redefine our understanding of the world.

The two phrases together encapsulate a comprehensive view of progress, recognizing both the importance of gradual improvement and the potential for transformative change. They acknowledge that progress can occur in continuous increments from one generation to the next, as well as through dramatic leaps forward brought by a new generation.

New Generation Thinking

Today we are dealing with a language proliferation and production revolution on a scale not seen since the invention of the printing press. Generation Z isn’t the next generation in the history of industrial society, they are the first generation who was raised with the infinite question and answer box of the internet. They are a new generation of human minds, blessed with a freedom of information that past thinkers would have killed to be able to access. It might be true that the initial impact of social media and the internet on human intelligence will be a dumbing down and emotional weakening of society, but the select few who take these new tools and use them to develop their own minds will find their place among the greatest minds of human history. It will be these leaders who will hopefully create the new system that will pull us from our backslide toward systemic authoritarianism.

The “new generation” of thinkers and leaders that I envision are people who have fully taken advantage of the infinite content library at our fingertips. They will have expertise so unique, so niche, and so interdisciplinary that their ideas will inevitably be fresh and innovative. They will have a deep appreciation for the technology of our times, and think seriously about its implications. They will be information adventurers, and knowledge explorers— motivated and energized by learning and building as opposed to critiquing and destroying. They will be blessed with the kind of naïveté that allows them to circumvent the imposter syndrome that paralyzes the next generation of leaders, but the rationality and realism not to confuse utopianism for idealism. Dogma and Ideology will be not only repulsive to these leaders, they will seem nonsensical and childish, like cognitive pacifiers. These leaders will be ready to meet the chaos and absurdity of the universe head-on, with purpose and motivation instead of fear and anxiety. Their allegiance will be to human beings, not institutions, and they will swim perpendicular to the riptide of technology instead of tiring themselves out trying to swim backwards.

The progress we espouse when we seek to become the next generation of humans is not human progress. It is the progress of systems, institutions, of apparatus of all types. The word next implies that the changing of human generations takes place within systems, not outside of them. This progress clouds our judgment, weakens our relationship with ourselves, and subsequently with the divine. If we hold that there is more to life and consciousness than just a wrote biological and hierarchical predestination— which I believe we must if there is to be any hope or meaning to be found in the workings of the universe— then trying to make progress toward understanding the spiritual weakness of our institutions stands out as an imperative issue to be addressed in our time. We must ask if our systems value those intangibles like love, care, and community, that are central to the human experience, and not whether we are adhearing to abstract principals and ideologies that have been warped and manipulated by generation after generation of industrial leaders. The progress we make within an inhuman bureaucratic system is a false progress, a step toward dystopia masquerading as justice. It is a biological— and thus meaningless— progress. It is a blind march toward the human superorganism and the final irrelevance of the human as an individual being.

The last time the West was presented with a technological revolution in language proliferation analogous to the internet, we saw immediate religious, political and scientific revolutions. The Protestant Reformation, the Enlightenment, the American Revolution, all occurring in quick succession. New mediums of content rapidly changed the thoughts, feelings, and behaviors of all English speakers throughout the world. And while we grapple with the proliferation revolution, we will bear witness to a language production revolution impossible to find analogy for in the history of human technology. Generative models will soon surpass human capabilities in many languages, and that is likely only the first of many radical shifts that intelligent technology will hoist upon human societies in the coming decades. All the answers about what to do now cannot be found in the 90 years of precedent that the next generation of leaders knows by heart, we must scour the memes of our entire history as a species to understand what it means to be human, and how to translate that into statecraft and policymaking. As I wrote in the conclusion to my Post History essay “If we are writing the last chapter of history as we know it, before handing over the reigns to machines that understand more than we could possibly imagine, it’s up to us to save our descendants from subjugation and provide for them the same benefits of freedom and self-determinism that have been gifted to us by our ancestors.”

My page is a challenge to you all, particularly the young and open-minded of America, to fill the yearning void of new generation leaders in culture today. Will you be the kind of person who responds to Anthony Fauci’s Message to the Next Generation of Scientists, or aspires to Time Magazine’s Next Generation of Leaders list? Or will you be the kind of person who responds to John F. Kennedy’s inaugural address when he says.

To borrow and adapt the opening words from Thomas Paine: “It matters not where you live, or what rank of life you hold, the evil or the blessing of the internet will reach you all.” To borrow and adapt Kennedy’s words: “Let the word go forth from this time and place, to friend and foe alike, that the torch has been passed to a new generation of Americans—raised in this century, tempered by screens, disciplined by surveillance, proud of our ancient heritage—and unwilling to witness or permit the slow creep of oligarchic technocracy to which this nation has always been opposed.” And to borrow some words from probability, I leave you all with a conclusion written by GPT-4.

Your challenge is clear - to become stewards of this new era, to harness the power of technology to uphold human values and rights in the face of an increasingly digital world. Arm yourself with knowledge, hold fast to empathy and, above all, remember that your unique voice is your most powerful tool in shaping this brave, new world. This, indeed, is the calling of the new generation.
